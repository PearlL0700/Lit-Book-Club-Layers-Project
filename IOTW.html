IOTW

Throughout God of the Woods, Liz Moore uses the evolving motifs of the woods, camp safety rules, performance, hunger, and concealed evidence to reveal that danger does not originate in the natural world but in the social systems meant to protect children. As setting, tone, and characterization shift across these motifs, moments that appear harmless become sites where adults misinterpret, neglect, or actively distort the truth. Moore demonstrates that harm arises when institutions prioritize image over honesty, routine over responsibility, and self-preservation over care. The novel ultimately argues that genuine safety comes not from rules, rituals, or appearances but from adults who are willing to see children clearly, respond with integrity, and confront the uncomfortable realities they have long avoided.
